Design and Simulation of an Astro-Photography Tripod with Hydrogel Actuator

The What and The Why

The objective of this thesis was to design and model a tripod mechanism for astrophotography, incorporating a hydrogel actuator to counteract the earthâ€™s rotation and provide the necessary actuation for long-exposure photography. The inspiration stemmed from the shortcomings of conventional tripod mounts, which necessitate additional, often heavy and expensive mounts to maintain stability during long exposures. This study sought to leverage the unique properties of smart materials, particularly hydrogels, which can act as actuators by swelling and deswelling in response to external stimuli, thus offering a compact and integrated solution.

The How

The project involved several key steps and utilized various software tools:

  • Literature Review and Conceptual Design: Conducted a comprehensive review of literature on hydrogels. Developed the initial conceptual design inspired by the Dobsonian telescope mount.
  • Analytical Modelling: Calculated dimensions and mechanical properties required for the hydrogel actuator using the Normalized Extended Temperature Expansion Model (NETEM).
  • Finite Element Modelling and Simulation: Developed a finite element model of the actuator on Abaqus. Simulated the actuator's behavior under applied loads and thermal stimuli.
  • Computer-Aided Design (CAD): Designed the outer shell and the complete tripod mechanism assembly on SolidWorks.
  • Prototyping: Designed the prototype to accommodate the actuator and ensure efficient actuation. Tested the prototype design virtually to assess its feasibility and performance.

The Outcome

The simulations showed that the designed hydrogel actuator could theoretically achieve the required actuation length under the specified conditions. However, several limitations were identified:

  • Response Time: The hydrogel's slow swelling kinetics resulted in impractically long response times for the intended application.
  • Mechanical Properties: The need for large dimensions to achieve stability led to a design that was aesthetically and ergonomically poor.
  • Actuation Rate: The swelling behavior of the hydrogel did not match the required strain rate for smooth and continuous actuation.

Despite these challenges, the project served as a proof-of-concept, demonstrating the feasibility of using hydrogels for actuation in astrophotography tripods. It paved the way for further research to optimize hydrogel properties for practical applications.

Learnings

Through this project, I developed both technical and soft skills. Technically, I gained expertise in finite element analysis (FEA) using Abaqus, CAD design with SolidWorks, and a deep understanding of the properties and behavior of hydrogels, particularly PNiPAAm. Additionally, I honed my skills in analytical modeling and deriving complex equations for smart materials. On the softer side, I improved my problem-solving abilities, conducted extensive research, and effectively communicated complex scientific concepts to a diverse audience. I also enhanced my project management skills by balancing multiple tasks and deadlines while maintaining a high level of quality and rigor.
