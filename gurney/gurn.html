Investigating the Effectiveness of Gurney Flaps for NACA 2412 Airfoil

Aerodynamic Performance at High Angle of Attack

The What and The Why

This project explores the aerodynamic impact of Gurney flaps on the NACA 2412 airfoil, particularly at high angles of attack. The Gurney flap is a simple trailing-edge modification that enhances lift, making it valuable in various aerospace applications. Understanding its behavior helps optimize airfoil performance, aiding in aircraft and racing car design.

The How

  • Modelling: Using OpenVSP and FreeCAD, the airfoil models of NACA 2412 profile were generated, incorporating Gurney flaps of different heights (1%, 2%, and 4% of chord-length).
  • Simulation Setup: The setup and simulations were carried in FreeCAD's CfdOF workbench, acting as GUI for cfmesh and Openfoam. Simulations were conducted for an angle of attack of 16Â°, an inlet velocity of 6 m/s keeping the Reynolds number at 400,000. Inflation layers were added on the airfoil surface to maintain y+< 1, and the number of volume cells varied slightly between configurations to ensure accurate results.
  • Execution: Steady-state simulations were conducted using kOmegaSSTLM turbulence model till convergence was observed in force monitors as well as residuals.
  • Analysis:
    • Lift and Drag Forces: Averaged the lift and drag force values over the last 200 iterations to ensure accuracy.
    • Efficiency Calculations: Calculated the lift-to-drag ratio (L/D) to determine aerodynamic efficiency.
    • Visualization: Used flow streamlines, pressure distributions, and velocity contours to visualize and compare aerodynamic effects due to different Gurney flap heights.

The Outcome

  1. Gurney flaps significantly enhance lift due to wake vortex formation, shifting the Kutta condition enforcement point downstream.
  2. Lift increases progressively with flap height, but so does drag, reducing the overall efficiency (L/D ratio).
  3. The Gurney flap of 2% chord-length height offers a good balance, maximizing lift while limiting the drop in L/D ratio, making it an optimal choice for lift enhancement.

Learnings

This project deepened my understanding of aerodynamic control surfaces, particularly their role in aerospace and automotive applications. By analyzing the influence of Gurney flaps on airfoil performance, I explored how trailing-edge modifications impact lift and flow control, knowledge that directly applies to real-world aerodynamic optimization. Additionally, I gained hands-on experience with an open-source CFD pipeline using OpenFOAM, refining my ability to conduct simulations in a resource-efficient manner. Structuring this study as a parametric investigation reinforced my specialization in CFD, enabling me to translate theoretical concepts into applied analysis.
