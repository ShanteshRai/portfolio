A Comprehensive CFD Study of the C130 Aircraft Wing

Winglet Effects and Propeller-Wing interaction Analysis

The What and The Why

This project is a comprehensive Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) study of the C130 aircraft wing, primarily focusing on the effects of winglets and propeller-wing interactions. The study comprises of following three sub-projects:

  1. CFD Analysis of C130 Aircraft Wing: The Winglet Effect - Analyzes and compares the aerodynamic performance of a wing with and without a winglet.
  2. CFD Analysis of Wing-Propeller Interaction in C130 Aircraft - Examines the aerodynamic interaction between the wing and propeller slipstream for standard and wingtip-mounted propeller configurations.
  3. Enhancing Aerodynamic Efficiency of a Wing: Winglet v/s Wingtip-Propeller - Compares the aerodynamic efficiency of a wing with a winglet versus a wingtip-mounted propeller.

The study aimed to optimize the aerodynamic efficiency of the C130 aircraft wing by reducing induced drag, and evaluating the potential benefits of wingtip-mounted propellers, especially in the context of modern and future aviation trends, including electric and hybrid-electric aircraft.

The How

  • Modelling:
    • Winglet Analysis: Created 3D CAD models of the wing with and without winglets using OpenVSP.
    • Wing-Propeller Interaction: A propeller was modelled on OpenVSP and the wing models were reused. Assembled the wing and propeller blades for both standard and wingtip-mounted configurations on Simscale. Used AMI (Arbitrary Mesh Interface) rotating zones to simulate propeller motion.
  • Simulation Setup: The setup and simulations were carried on the Simscale platform. Boundary conditions were set for cruise conditions, including an angle of attack of 3Â°, an inlet velocity of 66.9 m/s, a flight altitude of 1000 ft, and a propeller rotational velocity of 1020 RPM. Inflation layers were added on the wing and propeller blades to maintain y+< 3, and the number of volume cells varied slightly between configurations to ensure accurate results.
  • Execution: For the Winglet Analysis, steady-state simulations were conducted. For the Wing-Propeller Interaction, transient simulations with a time-step size of 0.0001 seconds for 5000 steps were performed.
  • Analysis:
    • Lift and Drag Forces: Averaged the lift and drag force values over the last 50 iterations or timesteps to ensure accuracy.
    • Efficiency Calculations: Calculated the lift-to-drag ratio (L/D) to determine aerodynamic efficiency.
    • Visualization: Used flow streamlines, pressure coefficient distributions, and velocity contours to visualize and compare the aerodynamic effects of different configurations.

The Outcome

  1. Winglet Effect:
    • Winglets reduced induced drag and improved fuel efficiency but increased parasite drag and weight.
    • Lift increased by 5.85%, but drag also increased by 44.07%, reducing overall aerodynamic efficiency by 26.5%.
  2. Wing-Propeller Interaction:
    • Wingtip-mounted propellers significantly reduced vortex formation and induced drag, improving aerodynamic efficiency by 46.84%.
    • Standard propeller configuration increased lift by 29.97% but also increased drag by 56.99%, reducing efficiency by 17.18%.
  3. Winglet vs. Wingtip-Propeller:
    • Wingtip propeller configuration outperformed the winglet configuration, showing a 31.6% improvement in aerodynamic efficiency.
    • Wingtip-mounted propellers reduced wingtip vortex strength without the disadvantages of increased parasitic drag associated with winglets.

Learnings

This project provided valuable learning experiences in CFD simulation, aerodynamic principles, and data analysis. By modelling and simulating various configurations of the C130 aircraft wing, I gained advanced skills in using Simscale for both steady-state and transient scenarios, and in creating 3D CAD models using OpenVSP. I deepened my understanding of aerodynamic concepts of aircraft wings, including wing and propeller geometry, winglets, and propeller-wing interactions. I also gained insights into wing design and its geometry, improving my ability to interpret simulation results and visualize aerodynamic effects. This project enhanced my problem-solving skills, and explored innovative design solutions like wingtip-mounted propellers.
